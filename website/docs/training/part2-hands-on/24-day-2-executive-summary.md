# 24. Day 2 Executive Summary


Day 2 of the RSA Prime enablement, held in person at the Westfield Marriott on July 1, 2026, shifted decisively from Day 1's install-and-architecture foundation to the operational craft of running, securing, customizing, and troubleshooting Prime in the field. The day was again delivered in two recorded segments: a 3-hour 3-minute main session (Part 1) and a 17-minute closing module on branding and email templates (Part 2), both led by Ramesh Anand with the in-room team participating hands-on.

Where Day 1 answered *"what is Prime and how do I stand it up,"* Day 2 answered *"what will I actually touch during the life of an engagement."* The session moved through certificate lifecycle management (including the JKS-to-PEM transition and the Diffie-Hellman parameter requirement), the two runtime configuration files that carry every Quick Setup decision (config.sh and setenv.sh), the completion of the WPI story with the SSP on-demand password-generation feature, a tour of the Prime team's Docker-based development and deployment infrastructure, full build-outs of two suite components previewed on Day 1 — the Virtual Token Service (VTS) and the Prime Central Authentication Portal (PCAP) — and finally the branding/CSS customization and email template systems that make Prime look like the customer's own.

Two qualities distinguished the day. First, it was genuinely hands-on: nearly every topic was demonstrated live, and several demonstrations broke in instructive ways — the VTS PIN rejection traced through logs to a username mismatch and a software-only mode flag, and an extended PCAP SAML integration debug that traversed certificate paths, keystore generation, and ultimately a WAR-file version patch. The trainer treated each failure as curriculum, repeatedly returning to the day's refrain: *"logs are your friend."* Second, the day surfaced real operational war stories — most notably a live customer incident from the prior week in which a compromised AMS Bind password (written on paper, exploited internally to create rogue accounts) was remediated on an emergency call using exactly the config.sh procedure taught in the session.

Key outcomes and themes from Day 2 include:

- Certificate replacement is simple but full of traps. Prime now uses PEM-format certificates (three files per application: cert, chain, key); the Diffie-Hellman parameters block in each cert file must be preserved across replacements to avoid reintroducing known vulnerabilities; chains must be ordered lowest-intermediate-first to root; and legacy JKS customers require either JKS-format replacement or conversion.
- config.sh and setenv.sh are the operational nerve center. Every Quick Setup answer — ports, certificate references, keystores, encrypted bind credentials, SMTP — lands in these two per-application files; the bind-password change procedure (Security Console → encrypt → paste → restart) is the canonical field task.
- Restarts carry latent risk. A one-line change forces a full configuration re-read, so silently expired internal certificates (trust, SAML signing — which generate no notification) or unnoticed syntax errors surface at the worst moment. Field guidance: restart first to flush latent failures, then make the intended change.
- The WPI story is now complete end-to-end. The SSP generate-password button (enabled via the allowedUserProfileOperations property) issues a short-lived AD password on demand, with pattern-controlled generation (A/a/N/S syntax), AM acting as the LDAPS proxy, and the Bulk Update scheduler sweeping the temporary password away on its next run.
- Docker is Prime's future — driven by Wells Fargo. The trainer's containerized environment (MS/SSP/HTAP containers, one-time setup container, HAProxy, Redis session cache, MailHog) demonstrated near-instant scaling, restart-without-logout, and a build-deploy-restart development loop; Wells Fargo is the first customer deployment.
- VTS and PCAP moved from concept to buildable. VTS was built end-to-end (builder tool, MTLS certificate trust, secret file, live troubleshoot to success); PCAP was positioned as the sovereign, on-prem CAS equivalent — the federal/DoD answer where cloud is unavailable — with SAML/OIDC integration configured in am8config.xml.
- Branding and email templates are two-file jobs. Look-and-feel lives in common.css and style.css (changes are instant, no restart); every outgoing email is an HTML-plus-properties template pair mapped to operations in the SSP properties.
